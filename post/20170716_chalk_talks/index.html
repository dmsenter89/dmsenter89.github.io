Chalk Talks and Plots

Sun, Jul 16, 2017 conference, thoughts

The past few days I was at the Utah Math Bio Alumni Conference. It was great being back at my alma mater surrounded by so many talented mathematicians and scientists, but more on that next week when I’ll write about my conference experience (I’ll also be at SMB this week). What I want to write about today are some thoughts I’ve had on chalk talks. Prior to their talks a lot of presenters took a few minutes to reminisce about their experiences with Utah math bio. The quality of physiology group meetings came up several times. At one point, someone (I think it was Eric Cytrynbaum) mentioned how it was difficult nowadays to get students to use the board during group meetings when everyone has a laptop and uses powerpoints and computer-generated plots. That got me thinking and wondering when I’ve had my last “real” chalk talk. And it’s been a while. It’s definitely true in my experience that we’ve become very reliant on powerpoints and beamer presentations. And I have to say I miss chalk talks. When I was a senior at Utah I took an intro to applied math course with Dr. Cherkaev that required us to present a final project on a topic of our choosing. I remember being the only one in the course not using a powerpoint.

I like chalk talks and I think being good at chalk talks is a great skill to have. And it’s especially important for graduate students. We don’t have a lot of papers out yet, with us being early in our career and all. But we have ideas and enthusiasm. Actively writing your work down while you discuss it with peers is a great way to communicate that enthusiasm. And this is frankly one area where I feel that pure math students tend to have a leg up on applied students. Or at least the one’s I know. One-on-one meetings with their advisors tend to involve blackboard work or going over handwritten pages. Applied students? Open your laptop, show some code and or graphs, what have you. Not that there is anything wrong with that. It just leads to us having less practice.

One of the appeals of computer aided presentation is that we can use graphs and images. In science we often have cool images and nice graphs and we love showing them off. But whether or not it is important to show someone these particular visuals depends on what we’re trying to accomplish. I’m not arguing we should do chalk talks at conference presentations. I’m thinking more of informal settings such as lab meetings or just getting together with peers in the department lounge. In such informal situations, what I am generally trying to accomplish is give my peers a high level overview of what I’m currently doing, what challenges I may face and what results I got. And if I have plots, I don’t expect them to notice anything very precise. In general I’m trying to point out some qualitative behavior of interest. And that frankly does not require me firing up matplotlib or a draft of a paper I’m writing. Qualitative behavior lends itself to back-of-the-envelope plotting.
