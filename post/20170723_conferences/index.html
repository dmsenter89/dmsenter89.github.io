Sunset over Salt Lake City.

Math Bio in Utah - 2017

Sun, Jul 23, 2017 conference, new-faces, outdoors, smb

I’m back in North Carolina after an exciting week of conferences and meeting many new people working in mathematical biology throughout the world. It started with the Utah math bio alumni meeting in honor of Jim Keener’s 70th birthday, which I briefly mentioned last week, and was followed by SMB’s annual meeting. Both were held in Salt Lake City, UT. I really love being back in Utah, even if it’s only for a little while. It was a great place to live. And the humidity… I’d take 100 F in Utah over 85 F in North Carolina any day!

Math Bio Alumni

I was excited to see talks by Bob Guy and Tim Lewis. I heard of them in lab meetings but hadn’t met them before. By chance I wound up sitting next to Andrew Basinski on Friday. After a brief “you look familiar” we recognized each other; back when I was an undergraduate at the University of Utah he was the TA for a math bio class I took from Fred Adler. He used to work on ants and now is working on the dynamics of vaccines that can spread much like a disease, except instead of making you sick they keep you from becoming sick. That was pretty exciting. I got to know a number of people from Karin Leiderman’s group, namely graduate students Nicholas Danes and Michael Kelley, and undergraduate Alison Wallbank.

While the conference officially ended on Saturday, Sunday still had activities. We went to the Math Biology Research and Reatreat Center, aka the Keener cabin, in Big Cottonwood Canyon. From there we went on a 3 mile hike to Lake Solitude (pictured above). We concluded with a BBQ at the cabin.

SMB 2017

I have to say I am now quite a fan of SMB meetings. This week was amazing. I’m already excited for next year’s SMB in Sydney. A fun experience here has been my starting to recognize names and faces of people I’ve met at previous conferences. It’s a nice feeling. It nakes me feel more like I belong and less like a student out of his league. We had a nice group from UNC there, especially several of Greg Forest’s people: Jia Zhao, a postdoc starting at Utah State this fall; Tim Wessler and Caitlin Hult, graduate students of Dr. Forest starting their postdocs at Minnesota this fall; and Kelsey Gasior, a postdoc in Biology who has been collaborating with Dr. Forest’s team on some multiphase modeling in fungi. Jay Newby, one of Dr. Forest’s postdocs I hadn’t previously met was likewise present. Of course my advisor Laura Miller and labmate Anne Talkington were here as well. We also saw Maggie Myers again. She’s an undergraduate researcher from Rhodes College who is also associated with St. Jude Children’s Research Hospital. She gave a talk at BAMM this May and will be applying to graduate school this fall. Reminded me of my first trip to a conference - SIAM CSE in 2015. Although mine didn’t have me travel out of state and I only went once, I had a really good experience. It was also good to see Alex Hoover again. He’s a former graduate student of my advisor, Laura Miller, who is currently a postdoc at Tulane.

Monday morning started great for me with the session on neuromechanical locomotion. Several people from the Tulane workshop in January were there. One talk that I especially enjoyed was the one by Eatai Roth, who discussed moth’s handling of sensory feedback, specifically visual disturbance versus mechanical disturbance felt through the proposcis. The afternoon I went to the cell biology session. My favorite talk of that session was probably Richard Buckalow’s talk. The poster session was held on Monday evening at the Natural History Museum. It was a fantastic setup in the museum’s foyer. We had dinner and open wine & beer bar. I don’t know the exact number, but there were over 100 poster presenters present! It was a wonderful experience.

The evolution and plant ecology session was held on Tuesday morning. I was particularily impressed by Michael Cortez’ and Peter Kim’s talks. Peter’s talk focused on a model for the “grandmother effect”, which was an interesting model. Tusday we only had the morning sessions and a plenary, while the afternoon was reserved for field trips and exploration. For that reason we had a simpler boxed lunch that day, which turned out in my opinion to have actually been the best lunch of the week. It was really simple too: a mozzarella & tomato sandwich. The bread was excellent, the cheese sumptuous, and the tomatos just marvelous. After lunch my lab, including former member Alex Hoover, went on a hike. We wanted to go up to the “Living room” but we took a detour and wound up at a different spot instead. Despite that, the sunset up on the mountains was just beautiful. The header image for this post was taken during that hike.

There was a great session on the mathematical modeling of honeybees. on Wednesday. It was quite fascinating. I hadn’t heard any recent news regarding hive collapses since it’s been in the news a few years ago, so I was interested in hearing what has been going on with this. The session started off strong with Mary Myerscough discussing some of her ODE models on hive death. While only a minor part of the talk, I appreciated her pointing out that bees may have nutritional deficits due to being surrounded by monocultures. While it seems very reasonable and quite obvious once you point it out, I have never actually actively thought about bee nutrition. So that was fun. She is a very good speaker and her talk was quite engaging. I also really enjoyed two talks from the afternoon Ecology and Crime session: Joanna Sooknanan and Donna Comissiong discussed the crime problem in Trinidad and Tobego utilizing a modified SIR model to describe gang dynamics. They spent quite a bit of time describing the rise of crime and especially gang violence in Trinidad & Tobego. It reminded me of what I had read on the rise of crime in the US several decades ago. Like the US earlier, politicians in Trinidad & Tobego felt that a good way to address the rise in crime would be by substantially increasing prison sentences, a method of dealing with crime that ignores problems associated with increased prison terms such as gang recruitment in prison and the difficulty of adjusting to a “normal life” after release. The talk by Sara Mc Carthy was also quite fascinating. She discussed game theoretic approaches to security challenges, with applications to illegal logging.

The annual banquet and dance was held Wednesday evening. I loved it. They had great food, but I mostly enjoyed the dancing. I personally love ballroom dancing and I had the good fortune to dance with some fun fellow mathematicians. Good times were had :) .

Even though Thursday was the last day of the conference we had some great talks and spirits remained high. I thoroughly enjoyed both plenary talks, both Dushoff’s discussion of epidemiology and Mimi Kohl’s discussion of microorganisms swimming in turbulent flow. I had previously been to one of her talks so I was quite excited to see her plenary. She definitely didn’t disappoint. She was very animated and had some cool pictures and great science. Alex Hoover gave his talk in the morning fluid session.

Overall I have to say I met a lot of great people and I had a tremendous amount of fun. I can’t wait for SMB 2018!
