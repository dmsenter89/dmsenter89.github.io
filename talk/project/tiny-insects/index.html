Tiny Insect Flight

 

Winged insects are taxonomically diverse and inhabit virtually every terrestrial ecosystem on our planet. They range in size from the most minute insects, barely visible to the naked eye, to those with wingspans greater than 7 inches - larger than the palm of an adult human! With such diversity, insects provide a plethora of model organisms for investigating flight.

Students of biology are familiar with the fact that small bacteria tend to move differently than fish swim for example. This is because a swimming motion is not effective in moving you forward when you’re as small as a bacterium. But flagella and celia will in fact move you forward effectively at that size. This difference in behavior has to do with how the organism in question experiences the relative effects of inertial and viscous forces. One way of quantifying the relative importance of these effects is with the dimensionless quantity known as the Reynolds number; it is given by $$\mathrm{Re}= \frac{\rho u L}{\mu},$$ where $\rho$ is the density of the fluid1 in which the organism is moving, $u$ is the velocity of the fluid with respect to the organism, $L$ is the “characteristic length” of the organism, and $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity of the fluid. We and many of the animals that we are familiar with from every day life live in a “high Reynolds number world.” When we say high, we mean a value of Re in the thousands and tens of thousands and higher. Bacteria on the other hand live in a low Reynolds number world, with values on the order of $\mathrm{Re} \ll 1$. The intermediate Reynolds number world is less well studied, and that’s where some interesting stuff happens!

Very small insects, such as thrips and fairyflies, live in the intermediate Reynolds number world. While larger insects typically create lift by creating a leading edge vortex that remains attached to their wings, while a trailing edge vortex is created and shed from the wing with each stroke. The tiny insects on the other hand shed neither their trailing nor leading edge vortices, and instead engage in what is known as clap-and-fling (cf. here for some details). Another interesting aspect of the flight of the smallest fliers is that their wings show interesting adaptations. Most of us are familiar with the way bee wings look. Even fruitflies have a very similar wing structure. Very tiny fliers on the other hand will have relatively small wings with large bristles attached to them, as in the image below.

When gathering footage for previous work on clap-and-fling (Santhanakrishnan et al, 2014), we also happened to come across footage showing parachuting behavior. Active flight is energetically costly, especially if you want to go far. Passive flight behaviors are an integral part of long-distance travel; just think of large birds you may see circling on convection currents in the air. My work is on trying to understand the aerodynamics of such passive flight behaviors in tiny insects.
